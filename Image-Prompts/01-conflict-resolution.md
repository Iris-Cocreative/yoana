A flat editorial illustration for use as an icon. A single large oval or rounded vessel form occupies the center of the composition — like a stone, a seed pod, or an ancient bowl. It has a crack running through it: a single winding fracture that travels from the upper left to the lower right. The crack is not catastrophic — it is mended. In the style of Japanese kintsugi, the crack is filled with gold, glowing softly against the form. The repair is the most beautiful part of the object.

The vessel/form itself is rendered as a flat filled shape — no texture, no shadow — in deep warm vermillion (#e74a2d). The kintsugi crack and its branching hairline tributaries are drawn in warm gold (#f4c656), slightly wider at the seam and tapering toward the edges. The crack has two or three fine secondary hairlines that branch off organically — not symmetrical, not perfect.

A thin outer ring of the same gold surrounds the form loosely, like a halo or the circle of a community — not touching the form, just enclosing it.

Background: warm cream (#fff6cf), flat.

Color palette: vermillion red (#e74a2d) for the main form, warm gold (#f4c656) for the crack and outer ring, deep umber (#4a2418) for thin outline strokes. No other colors.

Style: flat editorial illustration. Clean but with slightly organic, handmade-feeling edges. References Japanese kintsugi aesthetics, contemporary social justice visual culture, and the idea that repair is not erasure — it is transformation. The broken thing is now more whole than it was before.

Mood: breakdowns become breakthroughs. What has been broken can be mended — and the mending is visible, beautiful, not hidden.

Avoid: two separate figures, handshake imagery, scales of justice, courtroom imagery, literal crack-in-ground, shatter-glass aesthetics, photorealism, gradients, 3D effects, shadows, clipart, corporate iconography, anything symmetrical and mechanical.
